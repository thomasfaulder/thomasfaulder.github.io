Health in North Queensland

Tropical disease

Dengue Fever

Transmission

  • Transmitted by Dengue mosquitoes which have small, frequent meals of blood.

Symptoms

  • Symptoms last 3-7 days.
  • Intense muscle ache and pain behind the eyes.
  • Muscle and joint pain.
  • Loss of appetite and diarrhoea.
  • Skin rash.
  • Extreme fatigue.

Management

  • Eliminating mosquito breeding sites, such as water containers, from homes.
  • Wiping surfaces clean to remove mosquito eggs.
  • Wearing long-sleeved, light coloured clothes (mosquitoes attracted to dark colours).
  • Avoid being outside during times of heavy mosquito infestation (early evening).

Ross River Virus

Transmission

Mosquitoes, in mangroves and fresh water.

Symptoms

  • Inflammation and pain in joints.
  • Rash.

Malaria

Transmission

Mostly imported cases from the South-West Pacific, Asia and Africa.

Mosquitoes.

Symptoms

  • 9 days to several years after disease is contracted.
  • High fever.
  • Headache.
  • Backache.
  • Nausea.
  • Vomiting.
  • Muscle and joint pain.

Treatment

Anti-parasite and antibiotics.

Prevention

  • Anti-malarial drugs
  • Use insect repellents containing diethyl toluamide (DEET).

Indigenous Health Issues

Trachoma

Australia is the only developed country with trachoma.

Eye infection from Chlamydia trachomatis. Eyelids turn inwards so eyelashes rub against eyeball. Painful and causes blindness.

Transmission

Direct contact with fluid from eyes or noses of infected people. Flies that have contacted eyes can spread the disease. Bacteria can survive on towels and face cloths.

Management

  • Surgery (turning eyelids).
  • Antibiotics (kill Chlamydia trachomatis).
  • Education - teaching hand and facial cleanliness,ensuring families don't share towels.

Rheumatic heart disease

98% of Australians diagnosed with rheumatic heart fever are Indigenous or Torres Strait Islanders, because Indigenous communities lag behind in access to health care and general living standards.

Cause

Rheumatic fever causes the heart to dilate, stretching and scarring heart valves. Subsequently, blood flow is abnormal (eg. blood flows backward through valves), eventually leading to heart failure.

Management

  • Diagnosed with ultrasound.
  • Surgery can manage problems.
  • Significant decline due to
    1. Improved health care access for treatment.
    2. Penicillin has treated streptococcal infections which cause rheumatic fever.
    3. Improved living standards.

Symptoms

  • Chest pain.
  • Breathlessness with physical activity or lying down.
  • Weakness and tiredness.
  • Swelling of legs and face.

Respiratory diseases (eg. asthma)

Airways irritated so they become inflamed, narrowed, swollen and sometimes blocked by mucus, making it hard to breathe. Twice as common for Indigenous Australians than non-Indigenous Australians. Threatens quality of life.

Risk factors

  • Obesity - Indigenous Australians 1.6 times more likely to be obese than non-Indigenous Australians.
  • Smoking - smoking rates twice as high for Indigenous Australians.

Mental health problems

Causes

  • Disadvantage.
  • Discrimination - Indigenous may feel ignored, disrespected and misunderstood by non-Indigenous.
  • Grief and trauma stemming from feelings of loss from incarceration, illness, premature deaths and the Stolen Generation.

Possible solutions

  • Teach Aboriginal culture and history in schools to help Aboriginal children develop cultural identity and pride.
  • Educating Aboriginal perspectives on health and wellbeing- eg. strong focus on the importance of family and community.
  • Ensuring mainstream health services are culturally informed.
  • Giving Aboriginal communities more control over planning, designing and implementing health services to bring Aboriginal expertise and experience into the health care system and establish trust.
  • Having more Aboriginal health workers so Indigenous patients feel understood and respected.

Heart Disease

Includes Coronary Heart Disease (restricted blood supply to heart muscle caused by the build-up of fatty deposits in the coronary arteries) which can lead to heart attack. Indigenous Australians are twice as likely to be affected by cardiovascular disease than non-Indigenous Australians.

Risk factors

  • Smoking - 2/5 Indigenous people smoke.
  • High blood pressure - heart works harder and becomes thicker.
  • Diabetes - Aboriginal Australians 3x more likely to have diabetes.

Chronic kidney disease

Kidneys filter waste from the blood. If the kidneys begin to fail, waste and toxins accumulate.

Risk factors

  • Heart attack (blood supply to heart blocked) or stroke (blood supply to brain interrupted).
  • Overweight.
  • Smoking.

Outcomes

May lead to kidney failure. Patients who experience kidney failure may need dialysis or a kidney transplant.

Prevention

  • Healthy eating and regular exercise.
  • Not smoking.

Conclusion

Many Indigenous health issues stem from social disadvantage. Progress in education and housing could drive significant improvements in Aboriginal health.
